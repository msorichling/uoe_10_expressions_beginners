Green Hydrogen: The Energy of the Future

[1] Green hydrogen is a key component in the global effort to [1] **tackle climate change**. It is produced by using renewable energy sources, like solar or wind power, to split water into hydrogen and oxygen through a process called electrolysis. The major advantage is that this process creates no greenhouse gas emissions, making it a [1] **zero-carbon fuel source**.

[2] Unlike wind or solar power, hydrogen can be stored and transported, offering a solution to one of renewable energy's biggest problems: intermittency. It can be used to [2] **decarbonize heavy industry** and long-distance transport, areas which are difficult to electrify. For example, hydrogen can fuel large ships, trains, and even airplanes, providing a [2] **viable alternative** to fossil fuels.

[3] However, the technology still [3] **faces major hurdles**. Currently, producing green hydrogen is more expensive than using fossil fuels, and the infrastructure for transport and storage is still in its [3] **early stages of development**. To make it competitive, large-scale investment in renewable energy capacity and better electrolysers is needed. The aim is to lower production costs quickly.

[4] If these challenges can be overcome, green hydrogen has the potential to become a cornerstone of the future energy system. Governments and energy companies worldwide are now [4] **pouring billions into research** and pilot projects. This global commitment suggests that hydrogen will soon [4] **play a pivotal role** in creating a completely sustainable global economy.
